# Data Literacy column

**What this column is.** Data Literacy is interrogating a claim. Someone asserts something, and the move is finding what they are not telling you. The adversary is an *argument*, and the job is to catch it. 

**The three-tier arc.** Difficulty climbs by how deep the deception is buried, and it maps onto the three acts:

- **Act 1 (Rows 1-2): the presentation lies.** The data is fine; the display or the wording deceives. You catch it by looking harder at what is on the page.  
- **Act 2 (Rows 3-4): something is absent.** The data or a needed reference is missing, and you have to notice the absence, not just read what is shown.  
- **Act 3 (Rows 5-6): the inference or the origin is corrupt.** The data is real and fairly shown, but the conclusion drawn from it does not follow, or the finding is an artifact of how it was produced.

## Row 1: The visible distortion

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** Shadi is trying to sell you one of two cars. He shows you this and says: *'Both run at 800 RPM on average while parked, identical engines, both perfectly healthy.'* \[See Build Note for chart specs\] What's Shadi not telling you?  **The answer:** The engines are not identical. Car A's speed is steady (a healthy engine). Car B's swings violently, which is what a failing engine does when cylinders misfire. The average hides the variation. Car B is dying, and Shadi quoted a true number that conceals it. |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** Recognizing that the average conceals the difference, and identifying that Car B's readings swing wildly while Car A's are stable, so B is the unhealthy one. Saying "they're not identical" alone does not clear the gate; the diagnosis, that the average hides the spread, is the gate. **How they might get there**: Ignore the average and read the two rows. Car A barely moves; Car B is all over the place. An engine that cannot hold a steady speed while parked is misfiring or failing, so despite the matching average, B is in trouble. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**: "Shadi gave you the average. Look at the actual numbers behind it. Do the two cars look the same second to second?" "Car A stays near 800 the whole time. What is Car B doing? What would make an engine's speed jump around like that?" **Takeaway:** A healthy engine runs almost flat while parked, wobbling only about 50 to 100 RPM. A failing one surges and dips as cylinders misfire. Shadi's single number was incomplete and concealed what you would want to know before buying. This is the most common data trick there is: quote a mean, hide the range. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: The gentlest interrogation move. The claim is technically true (both really do average 800\) and rhetorically false (the cars are nothing alike). Everything you need is on the page, both the averages and the actual readings, so catching it is looking past the summary to the numbers underneath. No hidden data. The flaw is visible the moment you read the chart instead of the sentence. **Build note:** Car A, engine speed (RPM) while parked, over 10 seconds: 790, 810, 795, 805, 800, 800, 810, 790 *(average: 800\)* Car B, engine speed (RPM) while parked, over 10 seconds: 500, 1150, 600, 1050, 450, 1200, 400, 1050 *(average: 800).* Two line charts sharing one identical y-axis (critical — independent scaling kills the contrast). Dashed line at 800 on both. Car A flat, Car B a zigzag. Chart is honest, so no corrected version. |

## Row 2: The misleading comparison

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** Café de Casa, a Brazilian chain, has two locations in San Francisco. The Fisherman's Wharf location seats a maximum of 80 people; the Castro location seats a maximum of 50\. In one day, Fisherman's Wharf serves 512 people and Castro serves 400\.  The Castro manager claims his location serves more diners per seat than Fisherman's Wharf, and that he therefore deserves a raise. Assuming both locations have the same hours and diners spend about the same time eating, is he correct? **The answer:** Yes, he is correct. Per seat, Fisherman's Wharf serves 512 divided by 80 \= 6.4 diners, while Castro serves 400 divided by 50 \= 8 diners. Castro turns over more customers per seat, so relative to its size it satisfies more demand. |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** Recognizing that raw counts are not a fair comparison because the locations differ in size, and dividing by seats to get the per-seat rate (6.4 vs 8). The diagnosis, that you must adjust for capacity, is the gate. **How they might get there**: Notice the locations are different sizes, so raw totals are not comparable. Divide each by its seats: 512 / 80 \= 6.4, 400 / 50 \= 8\. Castro's higher per-seat number means the manager is right, even though his total looks smaller. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**:  "Fisherman's Wharf serves more people total. But it's also bigger. Is 'total served' a fair way to compare two differently sized places?" "Put each location's customers next to its seats. How many diners per seat does each one handle?" **Takeaway**: 2  true numbers can point the wrong way if they are not put on equal footing. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: A step past Row 1\. Nothing here visually screams; all four numbers look reasonable, and the raw totals (512 beats 400\) make it look like Fisherman's Wharf is the busier location. The move is to ask whether the two locations are compared fairly, given they are different sizes. Everything you need is on the page; you just have to relate the counts to the seats instead of taking totals at face value. **Other notes:** Unlike most Data Literacy questions, the claimant here is *correct*. The trap is the player's own too-quick reading (totals favor Fisherman's Wharf), and scrutiny *vindicates* the claim. This teaches that interrogating a claim does not always mean debunking it; sometimes it confirms it, and the thing to get past is the naive headline number. |

## Row 3: The missing denominator

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** You're at Safeway and your favorite yogurt, Yoplait, has a new label: *'1,000 people prefer us over Chobani.'* Using math, prove why this is misleading.  **The answer:** The label is meaningless without context that is missing: No denominator. If 1,200 were asked and 1,000 preferred Yoplait, that is \~83%, a real result. If 100,000 were asked and only 1,000 preferred Yoplait, that is 1%, meaning \~99% actually preferred Chobani, and the label hides a landslide *against* Yoplait. No opposing count. The label never says how many preferred Chobani, so "1,000 prefer us" is compatible with millions preferring the competitor.  |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** Identifying at least one of the two missing pieces (the total surveyed, or the count preferring Chobani) and, for the denominator, ideally showing *why* it matters by demonstrating that the same 1,000 supports opposite conclusions depending on the total. **How they might get there**: Notice the label gives a count with no total. Then test what the total could be: 1,000 out of 1,200 is impressive; 1,000 out of 100,000 is a disaster for Yoplait. Since the same number supports opposite conclusions, the claim is empty without the denominator. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**:  "1,000 people prefer Yoplait, out of how many who tried both? Does the label tell you?" "Imagine they asked 1,200 people. Now imagine they asked 100,000. Does '1,000 preferred us' mean the same thing in both cases?" **Takeaway**: This is one of the most common tricks in advertising and headlines: quote an impressive-sounding raw number and omit what it is a fraction of. The missing denominator is the single most useful thing to look for in any statistic. Also, to be able to compare to competitors, the data on competitors must be available. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: The first rung where the flaw is not on the page, it is a hole in the page. Nothing about "1,000 people" is false, and it sounds like a lot. But to know whether it means anything, you need a number they did not give you: out of how many people asked? That is the step up from Row 2, where the correcting number was printed. Here you have to notice it is missing, which you cannot do by reading harder. The prompt deliberately says "prove," echoing the discipline of the whole game: it is not enough to assert a claim is misleading; you have to show it. At this rung it is fine to tell players there is a gap; finding what it is is still the work. |

## Row 4: The misleading axis

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** Your friend claims that Damascus has about 2 to 3 times as many people as the other Levantine capitals. He shows you a chart to settle it. \[See Build Note for chart specs\] Is he right? **The answer:**  No. The numbers are close: Amman \~2.27M, Beirut \~2.40M, Damascus \~2.69M. Damascus has only about 1.18 times as many people as Amman, not 2 to 3 times. The chart fakes the gap by starting the y-axis at 2,250,000 instead of 0\. Drawn from a zero baseline, all three bars are tall and nearly equal. |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** Recognizing the y-axis does not start at zero and that this manufactures the gap, plus checking the actual ratio (\~1.18x, nowhere near 2 to 3x). Answering "yes" is the trap. **How they might get there**: The tell is Amman: its bar is nearly invisible, yet it represents 2.27 million people. That mismatch signals a chopped axis. Check the floor (2,250,000), then test the ratio: 2,685,000 / 2,273,000 ≈ 1.18, so Damascus is about 18% bigger, not 2 to 3x.  |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**:  "Where does the vertical axis start? What happens to the bars if it doesn't start at zero?" "Do the math on his actual claim: divide Damascus's number by Amman's. Is it really 2 to 3 times?" **Takeaway**: The truncated y-axis is the single most common chart trick in real media, precisely because it is invisible unless you look for it. It is not always dishonest (sometimes you legitimately want to zoom in), but it becomes a lie the moment it implies a huge gap where the real difference is minor.  Footnote: these are real metro-area figures (2024 to 2025\) but approximate, and Damascus's is especially uncertain because of wartime displacement, a small lesson in itself about trusting precise-looking numbers. **Host Note**: The chart deliberately omits Jerusalem because its population has no single apolitical figure – it depends on contested choices (which municipal boundaries, whether East Jerusalem's residents are counted as part of an Israeli city), so the number itself encodes a political position. If it comes up, use it: a guest catching the omission is the skill working in real time. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: The chart looks clean and persuasive, no visible contradiction like Row 1 had. The move is to notice what the chart is not showing: the y-axis does not start at zero. Starting it at 2,250,000 chops off almost the entire height of every bar and turns a small difference into an apparent landslide. That is an absence to catch, not a distortion you can see, which is what makes it Act 2\. And the claim is now specific ("2 to 3 times"), so you have to check the ratio against the labels to debunk it. **Build note:** Same three bars on both charts. Chart, 'Population of Levantine Capitals': three bars, y-axis running from 2,250,000 to 2,700,000. Bars labeled: Amman, Jordan, 2,273,000; Beirut, Lebanon, 2,402,000; Damascus, Syria, 2,685,000. Damascus's bar towers over the others; Amman's is a thin sliver. Question side: y-axis floor 2,250,000 (this truncation is the deception — do not start at zero). Answer side: floor 0, bars now near-equal. Both required. |

## Row 5: Correlation is not causation

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** In 2011, the NYPD detained, questioned, and conducted pat-downs on 685,724 people, invoking the stop-and-frisk practice allowed under reasonable suspicion. That same year, New York City's violent crime rate was at lows the city hadn't seen in decades. The city's then-mayor, Michael Bloomberg, made the case that aggressive stop-and-frisk was taking guns off the street and driving crime down, and that pulling back on it would put the city in danger. The stops were high and crime was low. What additional information, if any, do you need to prove stop-and-frisk caused the drop? **The answer:** Two things moving together does not establish that one caused the other. What you would need is some way to see what crime would have done *without* the stops: A comparison group. A U.S. city without stop-and-frisk and the rates of crime over the same period of time.  A removal test. Crime rates after the removal of the stop-and-frisk policy |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** The gate is recognizing that high-stops-plus-low-crime cannot prove causation and naming what would be needed to test it: (1) a comparison group (crime where there was no such policy) or a removal test (what happened when stops ended). "Correlation isn't causation" as a bare slogan does not clear it; a specific test or alternative explanation does. Answering "no additional information needed, the correlation proves it" is the trap. **How they might get there**: Separate the two facts (stops high, crime low) from the claimed link between them, then ask what would let you see the counterfactual. A bigger trend affecting everyone or a test of removing the supposed cause each shows the causal story does not hold. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**:  "To prove the stops caused it, you'd want to compare against a world without the stops. Where could you find something like that, another city, or a later time when the stops ended?" "If crime fell just as much in places that never used stop-and-frisk, or kept falling after New York abandoned it, would his claim still stand?" **Takeaways**: When someone says A caused B because they happened together, ask what else was going on, and ask what happened when A went away. **Comparison group.** Crime was falling across the entire US, including in cities without stop-and-frisk, where crime fell just as much. **A removal test.** After a 2013 court ruling found the program unconstitutional, stops collapsed, but crime didn’t surge; it kept falling. **Racial bias.** In 2011, about 84% of those stopped were Black or Latino. Years later, with a fraction of the stops, the share of stopped people who were Black actually rose (59% of stops against a 24% population in 2022). Whether the program was large or small, it fell on the same people. **Host note:** The defensible claim is that the correlation does not establish causation, and the fuller data (national decline, plus crime continuing to fall after stops ended) shows the causal story is unsupported. Avoid overstating it as "proven to do nothing," a stronger claim than the data licenses; the game's own credibility depends on not overclaiming. The point is the reasoning error, not a verdict on every effect of the policy. The lesson is transferable, the same co-movement-is-not-causation error drives bad claims about diets, economics, and advertising, which is useful to name if the room's energy gets political. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: The step from Act 2 into inference. Nothing here is hidden or distorted; the numbers are real and fairly stated. What is on trial is the conclusion: two things moved together, and the claim is that one caused the other. You cannot catch this by finding a missing number or a chopped axis. You have to interrogate the leap from "these happened at the same time" to "this caused that," and identify what would actually test it. That is the hardest data-literacy move, which is why it anchors Act 3\. |

## Row 6: The manufactured finding

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** Egypt is convinced that playing the right song on his website will make more people buy what he sells. To find out, he tests 20 different songs, one at a time. For each song, he shows the site to about 100 visitors with the song and 100 without, and checks whether the song produced significantly more purchases. Differences are statistically significant if there's less than a 5% chance of seeing them by luck alone. 19 of the songs show nothing. But one, a track by Kali Uchis, comes back significant: noticeably more purchases, clearing the 5% bar. Egypt posts on Instagram: *'Kali Uchis boosts my sales.'* His calculations are all correct. So why should you distrust his post? **The answer:** The Kali Uchis result is almost certainly a fluke, manufactured by testing 20 songs and reporting the one that hit.  The 5% threshold means each song has a 5% chance of looking significant by luck. Test one song and a false alarm is unlikely. Test 20 and you expect about one false alarm on average (20 times 5% \= 1).  |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** Identifying that testing many songs and reporting only the winner manufactures the false positive, that the Kali Uchis result is expected noise from running 20 tests, not a real finding. **Tie-breaker:** when both teams clear the gate, the team that *shows the math* (20 tests times 5% ≈ 1 expected false positive by chance) wins over a team that reasons it only qualitatively.   **How they might get there**: Look at the design. The 5% threshold means a 1-in-20 chance of a false positive per test, even when nothing real is going on. Egypt ran 20 tests, so about one false positive was essentially guaranteed. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**:  "Forget Kali Uchis for a second. How many songs did Egypt test in total? If every song were completely useless, how many would you still expect to look 'significant' just by chance?" "The threshold allows a 5% chance of a false alarm per song. He tested 20 songs. 20 times 5% is what?" **Takeaway**: This is called p-hacking, or the multiple-comparisons problem, and it is one of the biggest reasons published science, especially in nutrition and health, keeps reversing itself. Coffee causes cancer; coffee prevents cancer; eggs are bad; eggs are fine. Many of these are single significant findings pulled from studies that quietly tested dozens of things. The defense is the same question the game has been teaching: not *is this fact true?* but *how did they find it, and what aren't they showing me?* |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: The finale, and the one question where fact-checking fails completely. Every prior question had a flaw you could point to: a hidden number, a chopped axis, a bad causal leap. Here, nothing is false. The data is real, the statistics are done correctly, the Kali Uchis result genuinely clears the bar. You can only catch it by asking how the finding was found, the deepest data-literacy move there is, and the reason bad science and bad marketing slip past even careful readers. **Other notes:** The subject is Egypt, the game's own designer, caught p-hacking his own website. That is deliberate. The whole game argues that anyone can fool themselves with numbers, including the person who built a game about not being fooled by numbers, which is the same humility the project's process documentation is built around. |
