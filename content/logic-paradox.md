# Logic / Paradox column

**What this column is.** Logic / Paradox is reasoning against your own faulty intuition. The answer is fixed and provable, but your gut fights you the whole way: your first instinct is confidently wrong, and getting to the truth means distrusting the obvious and following strict reasoning instead. 

*Note: Field schema, authoring rules, seam tests, and the difficulty ladder are in CLAUDE.md; player-experience and scoring notes are in README.*

## Row 1: The quick gotcha

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** You post a thirstrap on Instagram and get 100 likes. Some of them were bots. If the number of bot likes was 60 more than the number of real likes, how many real likes did you get? **The answer:** 20 real likes (and 80 bots). Check: 80 is 60 more than 20, and 80 \+ 20 \= 100\. The trap is that "60 more" makes the gut subtract 60 from 100 and say 40\. |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** The gate is landing on 20 with a correct account of why (the 60 is the difference, so you split what is left, not subtract 60 from 100). **How they might get there**: Resist the reflex (40) and test it: 40 real would need 100 bots to be "60 more," totaling 140, too many. So set it up properly: real \+ (real \+ 60\) \= 100, meaning two times real \= 40, so real \= 20\. Or intuitively: the 60 is the gap, so take it out (100 − 60 \= 40\) and split the rest evenly (20 each), then add the 60 back to one side (20 and 80). |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**: "Most people's first instinct is 40\. Before you commit, check it: if there were 40 real likes, how many bots would that be, and does it add up to 100?" "The 60 is the difference between bots and real likes, not the number of bots. So how do you split the rest?" **Takeaway:** Almost everyone's gut says 40 first. That is exactly the point, and it is the shared experience, not a failure. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: The gentlest Logic move. Your gut hands you a confident wrong answer, but the fix is immediate once you slow down. No multi-step reasoning, no probability, just "don't answer on reflex." The math is trivial; the whole challenge is that your brain grabs the wrong number. It sets the tone for the column: you are supposed to fall for these. **Reskin note**: This is the classic bat-and-ball problem, reskinned. The structure that must survive any further reskin: a round "more than" number sitting inside a total that is just above it, so the naive subtraction is tempting and the real answer is a surprising half of the small leftover. |

## Row 2: The overlooked case

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** Memo's shift at Badlands is from 10 PM to 3 AM. If during his shift the clocks fall back by 1 hour, for how many hours does Memo work? **The answer:** 6 hours. The clock only shows 5 (10 PM to 3 AM), but when it falls back, the hour between 1 and 2 AM happens twice, so Memo is there for 6 actual hours. |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** The answer 6, with the recognition that falling back means an hour repeats, so he works longer than the clock suggests. **How they might get there**: Do the naive count (10 PM to 3 AM \= 5 hours), then ask what "falling back" does. The clock rewinds, so the 1-to-2 hour is worked twice: 10-11, 11-12, 12-1, 1-2, 1-2 again, 2-3 \= 6 hours. The joke and the lesson: falling back costs Memo an extra hour of work, not less. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**: "When the clock falls back, does Memo get to leave earlier, or does he end up working an hour again?" "Trace it hour by hour: 10-11, 11-12, 12-1, 1-2, and then the clock jumps back to 1\. Now what?" **Takeaway:** Both 5 (ignoring the change) and 4 (treating "fall back" as subtraction) are the expected traps. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: A step past Row 1\. The gut answers thoroughly but incompletely: it does the clock arithmetic and either forgets the change or gets its direction backwards. "Falling back" sounds like losing an hour, so the instinct says he works less, when he actually works more, because the repeated hour has to be lived twice. The fix is not slowing down, it is tracing the shift hour by hour. **Authoring note**: An earlier draft ran 10 PM to 2 AM, which put the clock change exactly at the end of the shift, so the trap caught nobody and the answer was just 4\. The change has to fall inside the window. Always run the trap and confirm it fires. Also note the wording deliberately supplies both the direction and the size of the change ("fall back by 1 hour") rather than requiring players to recall US DST rules, since recall is not reasoning. |

## Row 3: The counting/probability trap

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** Lyon wants to draw the Siren card from a deck of 10 tarot cards. He draws one card, looks at it, and shuffles it back in. He does this 10 times. What are the chances he will draw the Siren at least once? **The answer:** About 65%. The chance he misses on any one draw is 9/10. Missing all ten in a row is 9/10 of 9/10 of 9/10, ten times over, which comes to about 35%. So he strikes out about a third of the time. |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** Landing near two-thirds (anywhere around 60-70% with sound reasoning) and, crucially, explaining the flip: that you count the chance of never drawing it and subtract.Answering \~100% is the expected trap. **How they might get there**: The key move is counting the misses instead of the hits. Chance of missing one draw: 9/10. Missing all ten: 9/10 of 9/10 of 9/10... ≈ 35%. Since "at least once" and "never" are the only two options, at least once \= 100% − 35% ≈ 65%. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**: "Forget 10 draws. What if he draws twice? Chance of missing both is 9/10 of 9/10, about 80%, so he wins at least once about 20% of the time, not from adding but from counting the misses." "'At least once' is messy to count directly (he could hit on draw 3, or 7, or both). But never is one clean story: miss, miss, miss, all ten times. Compute that.” **Takeaway**: Probability is counting your slice of all possible worlds. **Multiply for "and."** Each condition narrows what is left. Winning twice at 1-in-10 each is 1/10 of 1/10 \= 1/100. "Of" means multiply in plain English (half *of* a half is a quarter). **Add for "or," but only when the options cannot both happen.** Rolling a 1 or a 2 is 1/6 \+ 1/6, because you cannot roll both at once. The trap in this question is over-applying this: ten tries *can* overlap. **Flip when the question says "at least one."** "At least once" is many messy scenarios; "never" is one clean story. Since they are the only two possibilities, compute the easy one and subtract: at least once \= 100% − never. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: The first rung where you have to compute your way out of the trap. The gut says "1-in-10 chance, 10 tries, that's a sure thing" and it is badly wrong. The failure is not forgetting a case (Row 2), it is that intuition is terrible at judging how likely repeated chances actually are. Teams discuss here because arguing through why "guaranteed" is wrong is the work. |

## Row 4: The conditional-probability flip

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** Klinefelter Syndrome is a genetic condition in which a person is born with an extra X chromosome (XXY). It affects 1 in 1,000 people with a Y chromosome. The test for it is 99% accurate. In other words, it correctly identifies that someone does or does not have it 99% of the time. If a test comes back positive, what are the chances that someone with a Y chromosome actually has it? **The answer:** About 9%. Imagine 1,000 people with a Y chromosome: About 1 actually has it, and the test catches it. That is 1 true positive. The other 999 do not have it, but the test is wrong 1% of the time, which is about 10 false positives. Total positive results: 1 \+ 10 \= 11. Only 1 is real. |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** Landing near 9% (anywhere around 5-15% with sound reasoning) and explaining why: that the false positives from the large healthy group swamp the single true positive. **How they might get there**: Of 1,000: \~1 has it (true positive), \~10 false alarms from the 999 who do not. That is 11 positives, 1 real, so about 1 in 11\. The insight is that the test's accuracy is not the whole story; the base rate is. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**: "Forget percentages. Imagine 1,000 people with a Y chromosome get tested. How many actually have it? Now, of the ones who don't, how many does the test get wrong?" "What if the condition affected 1 in 10 people instead? That would mean \~100 real cases against only \~9 false alarms." **Takeaway**: Many people with Klinefelter are never diagnosed at all, and many who are only find out as adults, usually when they run into fertility problems. So the question is about false positives, and reality has the opposite problem too: a condition common enough to affect 1 in 1,000 mostly goes undetected. Testing statistics mislead in both directions. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: The hardest kind of gut-failure short of the finale. Row 3's error was misjudging a size. Here the error is refusing to believe that the rarity of the condition matters as much as the accuracy of the test. The gut hears "99% accurate" and answers "99%," and it stays uncomfortable even after you prove otherwise. This is the row where teammates argue with each other, and then argue with the answer. |

## Row 5: When the parts contradict the whole

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** In 1973, two graduate departments at UC Berkeley had these admissions numbers: Department Men Women A 512 admitted of 825 (62%) 89 admitted of 108 (82%) F 22 admitted of 373 (6%) 24 admitted of 341 (7%) Across the two departments combined, who was admitted at a higher rate? **The answer:** Men, about 45% compared to 25% for women. . Men: 512 \+ 22 \= 534 admitted of 1,198 applied \= \~45%. Women: 89 \+ 24 \= 113 admitted of 449 applied \= \~25%. Department A admits about 2 out of 3 applicants. Department F admits about 1 in 16\. Roughly 69% of these men applied to the generous department; roughly 76% of these women applied to the brutal one. The combined number is not measuring how anyone was treated. It is measuring which line they stood in. |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:** Explaining that most men applied to the easy department and most women to the hard one, so the combined rate reflects where people applied rather than how they were treated. Exact percentages are not required; the reasoning is the gate. Answering "women" is the expected trap. **How they might get there**: Spot the lopsided distribution first, then either add each column and divide (534 of 1,198 versus 113 of 449\) or reason it qualitatively: the men clustered where admission was generous, the women clustered where it was nearly impossible, so the combined number follows the departments. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**: "Don't just compare the rates. Look at how many men applied to each department, and how many women." "Department A lets in about two-thirds of everyone. Department F lets in about one in sixteen. If most men are in one and most women are in the other, what is the combined number actually telling you?" **Takeaway**: Berkeley was sued for sex discrimination in 1973: 44% of men admitted, 35% of women. Properly pooled, the data showed a small but statistically significant bias *in favor of* women.  So why were women in the crowded departments? The paper's own answer: the easier departments were the ones requiring **more undergraduate math**, and women in 1973 were far less likely to have been given it. They were choosing the doors they were qualified for. The bias was real. It just happened years upstream in who got taught math. |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: Row 4's gut error was refusing to accept that new information changes the odds. Here the gut is certain something is logically impossible: if women did better in every single department, they must do better overall. That feels like arithmetic itself. It is false, and it is provably false, which is what makes this a step up. The math is heavy because you cannot just compare rates, you have to weight them by how many people were actually in each group. **Reskin note**: Simpson's paradox breaks easily. What must survive any reskin: (1) two actors, two subgroups, one subgroup naturally easier; (2) actor A beats actor B in both subgroups; (3) lopsided distribution, where A does most of their work in the hard subgroup and B in the easy one (this is the engine); (4) a story reason for the lopsidedness; (5) equal or comparable totals, so the takeaway cannot be dismissed as "one of them just did more." After any edit, re-check that A still wins both subgroups. **Build note:** Render as a table (Department, Men, Women; rows A and F), figures preserved. No chart. |

## Row 6: When the group can't want anything

 

|  |  |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Board Content** | **The question:** Three voters are ranking three candidates: a Democratic Socialist, a Democrat, and a Republican.  Voter 1st 2nd 3rd Voter 1 Republican Democrat Democratic Socialist Voter 2 Democrat Democratic Socialist Republican Voter 3 Democratic Socialist Republican Democrat  Each voter has a clear, consistent ranking. The group votes head-to-head, majority rules. Who wins? **The answer:** There is no winner. Republican beats Democrat, 2-1 (Voters 1 and 3\) Democrat beats Democratic Socialist, 2-1 (Voters 1 and 2\) Democratic Socialist beats Republican, 2-1 (Voters 2 and 3\) The majority prefers R to D, D to DS, and DS to R. It is a loop. Every one of those is a legitimate 2-1 majority. |
| **Scoring** | **What earns the points:**  Recognizing there is no winner, and showing the cycle. The gate is whether they **checked the third matchup**. A team that checks R vs D, then D vs DS, and stops, concluding "Republican, obviously," fell for it, and not from laziness: transitivity *promised* them the third check was unnecessary.  **How they might get there**: Grind the three pairings by hand; it is counting to 2, three times. The difficulty is not the arithmetic, it is giving yourself permission to check a matchup that logic says is already settled. |
| **Host Notes** | **Hints**: "Run all three head-to-heads. All of them. Even the one you think you already know." "You've got R beating D, and D beating DS. So obviously R beats DS. Check it anyway." **Takeaway:** It gets worse. Suppose they vote in rounds: two candidates first, the winner faces the third. Put **Democratic Socialist against the Democrat first**: the Democrat wins. Then the Democrat faces the Republican, and loses. **The Republican takes it.** Now run it again with a different pair going first, and a different candidate wins. Every candidate wins under some order. Nobody cheated, nobody voted dishonestly, every round was a clean majority, and **whoever scheduled the votes picked the winner.** Condorcet published this in 1785\. In 1951, Kenneth Arrow generalized it: no voting system can satisfy a short list of obviously reasonable fairness conditions. He won a Nobel for it. This three-card puzzle grew into a proof that a perfect democracy cannot exist. **Host note:** Someone may object that nobody really ranks Democratic Socialist first and Democrat last. That voter is *mathematically required*: if all three ranked along a single left-right axis, the cycle is impossible and the Democrat simply wins as the median. Cycles need someone ranking on a second dimension, like establishment versus outsider. That voter is real: 12% of Sanders 2016 primary voters voted Trump in the general, per the Cooperative Congressional Election Study (a survey of about 50,000 people). |
| **Documentation** | **Why it's this level**: Row 5 told you a number meant something different than you thought. This one tells you the number does not exist. The gut is not just wrong, it is certain the result is impossible: if most prefer A to B, and most prefer B to C, then most prefer A to C. That is not a fact about voters, it feels like a law of thought. And it is false. Unlike every row before it, this one does not dissolve when you see the proof.  **Reskin note:** The three rankings must be cyclic rotations of each other. Change any single voter's ranking and the paradox vanishes entirely. Three voters and three options is the tightest version; scaling up gains nothing and loses the ability to check it on your fingers. The three options must be plausibly rankable in any order. **Build note:** Render as a table (Voter, 1st, 2nd, 3rd; three rows), exact rankings preserved — the cyclic order is load-bearing. No chart. |
